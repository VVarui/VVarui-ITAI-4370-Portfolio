# Reflective Essay 1: How My Understanding of Telecommunications Evolved

*[Edit this draft to reflect your own voice and experience before submitting — it's written from the labs' content, but the reflection should be genuinely yours.]*

When the course began, I thought of telecommunications mostly in physical terms: towers, cables, signals moving through the air. Lab 2, where I calculated Free-Space Path Loss for a 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi signal, was my first real encounter with *why* wireless networks are designed the way they are. Seeing path loss jump from about 80 dB at 0.1 km to nearly 94 dB at just 0.5 km made the logarithmic nature of signal degradation concrete rather than abstract — it explained, in numbers, why access point placement and coverage radius are such carefully engineered decisions rather than guesswork.

From there, my understanding shifted from "how signals travel" to "how networks behave over time." Labs 3 and 4 moved me from static physical-layer thinking into the operational side of telecom: predicting and managing traffic. Working with a full year of hourly traffic data taught me that networks have rhythms — daily cycles, weekly cycles, business-hours surges — and that these rhythms are exactly what a well-designed model should exploit. I hadn't previously thought about network traffic as something with predictable seasonality in the same way retail sales or weather have seasonality, but the exploratory data analysis and seasonal decomposition in Lab 4 made that pattern impossible to ignore.

By Lab 5, my perspective had broadened again — from *predicting* network behavior to *deploying* intelligence within the network itself. Learning about edge computing and model compression connected back to the physical constraints I'd first seen in Lab 2: just as a wireless signal loses power over distance, a machine learning model has to "lose weight" (through pruning, quantization, or distillation) to survive on a resource-constrained edge device. That parallel — physical signal constraints and computational resource constraints — was not something I expected to find, but it tied the course together for me.

Overall, my understanding evolved from viewing telecommunications as a fixed infrastructure problem to seeing it as a dynamic system: one where physical limits, traffic patterns, and computational constraints all interact, and where AI is increasingly the tool used to manage that interaction efficiently.
