# Reflective Essay 1: How My Understanding of Telecommunications

When the course began, I thought of telecommunications mostly in physical terms like towers, cables, signals moving through the air. Lab 2, where I calculated Free-Space Path Loss for a 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi signal, was my first real encounter with wireless networks are designed the way they are. Seeing path loss jump from about 80 dB at 0.1 km to nearly 94 dB at just 0.5 km made the logarithmic nature of signal degradation concrete.

From there, my understanding shifted from "how signals travel" to "how networks behave over time." Labs 3 and 4 goes in depth with the operational side of telecom: predicting and managing traffic. Working with a full year of hourly traffic data taught me that networks have rhythms and that these rhythms are exactly what a well-designed model should exploit. 
By Lab 5, my perspective had broadened again — from *predicting* network behavior to *deploying* intelligence within the network itself. Learning about edge computing and model compression connected back to the physical constraints I'd first seen in Lab 2: just as a wireless signal loses power over distance, a machine learning model has to "lose weight" (through pruning, quantization, or distillation) to survive on a resource-constrained edge device. 
Overall, my understanding evolved from viewing telecommunications as a fixed infrastructure problem to seeing it as a dynamic system: one where physical limits, traffic patterns, and computational constraints all interact, and where AI is increasingly the tool used to manage that interaction efficiently.
